What is DDH?

DDH is a spectrum of conditions where the hip joint does not develop normally. The socket (acetabulum) can be shallow, and the ball (femoral head) may be partially (subluxation) or completely out of the socket (dislocation). Early detection and age-appropriate treatment can restore normal function.

  • Spectrum: dysplasia → subluxation → dislocation
  • Common in infants and young children
  • Can be unilateral or bilateral
  • Untreated: limp, pain, early osteoarthritis

Why it matters

Healthy hips support standing, walking, running, and play. When the femoral head is well-seated in the socket, the acetabulum forms deeply. Instability prevents normal shaping and leads to long-term problems if ignored.

Early Detect Treat

Pathophysiology

DDH arises from an abnormal relationship between the femoral head and acetabulum. If the ball is not centered, the socket stays shallow and the hip becomes unstable. Over time, this can alter gait mechanics and accelerate joint wear.

Hormone sensitivity

Female infants are more sensitive to maternal hormones (e.g., relaxin, estrogen), creating ligamentous laxity and instability.

Breech positioning

Hips in extension/adduction press the femoral head on the rim of the socket, increasing risk up to 20×.

First-born & Genetics

Less uterine space and family history both raise risk.

Screening & Diagnosis

  • Newborn exam: Barlow (provocative) and Ortolani (reduction) maneuvers
  • Clues: asymmetric thigh folds, leg-length difference, limited abduction
  • Imaging: ultrasound (<6 months), X-ray (>6 months)
  • High-risk infants (e.g., breech) → targeted imaging even with normal exam

Age-Based Treatment

< 6 months · Pavlik harness

Worn 23–24 hours/day to hold hips flexed and abducted so the socket can form around the femoral head. High success when started early.

6–18 months · Closed reduction + spica cast

Under anesthesia, the hip is reduced and immobilized in a cast to maintain position while the acetabulum develops.

> 18 months · Open reduction ± osteotomy

Surgical reduction and, when needed, bone reshaping procedures help realign and stabilize the joint when earlier methods fail or diagnosis is delayed.

Case Study: Emma, 2 months

Emma was born breech. On exam, an Ortolani maneuver produced a palpable “clunk”. Ultrasound confirmed DDH. A Pavlik harness was applied and worn continuously. At 4 months, imaging showed a stable, concentrically reduced hip, and the harness was weaned successfully.

  • Risk: breech presentation
  • Finding: positive Ortolani
  • Outcome: stable hips with early treatment
