What is DDH?

DDH is a spectrum where the hip joint doesn‚Äôt develop normally. The socket (acetabulum) may be shallow, and the ball (femoral head) can be partially (subluxation) or completely out of the socket (dislocation). Early detection and age-appropriate treatment can restore normal function.

  • Spectrum: dysplasia ‚Üí subluxation ‚Üí dislocation
  • Common in infants and young children
  • Can be unilateral or bilateral
  • Untreated: limp, pain, early osteoarthritis

Why it matters

Healthy hips support crawl, stand, walk, and play. When the femoral head is well-seated, the socket deepens. Instability prevents normal shaping and leads to long-term problems if ignored.

Pathophysiology

DDH arises from an abnormal relationship between the femoral head and acetabulum. If the ball isn‚Äôt centered, the socket stays shallow and the hip becomes unstable. Over time this alters gait mechanics and accelerates joint wear.

Hormone sensitivity

Female infants may be more sensitive to maternal hormones (relaxin/estrogen), creating ligamentous laxity and instability.

Breech positioning

Hips in extension/adduction press the femoral head on the socket rim, increasing risk substantially in breech births.

First-born & Genetics

Less uterine space and family history both raise risk.

Screening & Diagnosis

  • Newborn exam: Barlow (provocative) and Ortolani (reduction) maneuvers
  • Clues: asymmetric thigh folds, leg-length difference, limited abduction
  • Imaging: ultrasound (<6 months), X-ray (>6 months)
  • High-risk infants (e.g., breech) ‚Üí targeted imaging even with normal exam

< 6 months ¬∑ Pavlik harness

Worn 23‚Äì24 hrs/day to hold hips flexed & abducted so the socket can form around the femoral head.

Under anesthesia the hip is reduced and immobilized to maintain position while the acetabulum develops.

Parent Guide

  1. Keep the harness on 23‚Äì24 hrs/day. Don‚Äôt adjust straps without guidance.
  2. Put the diaper under the harness; check skin daily for redness.
  3. Use hip-healthy swaddling: legs free to flex/abduct.
  4. Attend all ultrasound/X-ray follow-ups.
  5. Use teach-back: explain care steps in your own words to confirm understanding.
Tip: Take a photo of strap placement after each clinic visit.
